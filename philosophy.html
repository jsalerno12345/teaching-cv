Teaching Philosophy

How I think about the classroom — and why it works.

🔑

Math Is For Everyone

I don't believe in "math people." My job is to find the key that unlocks understanding for each student — whether they're in a developmental algebra course or pushing through a proof in Linear Algebra. Everyone can get there with the right approach.

⚖️

Rigor With Warmth

High standards and a supportive classroom aren't opposites — they reinforce each other. I hold students to real expectations while making sure they always feel safe to ask questions, make mistakes, and try again. Struggle is part of learning math, and I treat it that way.

🌐

Real-World Connection

I work professionally as a data engineer — I've used differential equations, statistics, and linear algebra on real problems with real stakes. I bring those examples into every course so students can see exactly why this material matters outside the classroom.

In My Own Words

I became a math teacher because I genuinely believe that mathematics is for everyone — not just the students who take to it naturally, but especially the ones who walk into class convinced they can't do it. Those are the students I find most rewarding to work with, and over more than a decade of teaching, they've shaped my entire approach to the classroom.

My teaching career began at Atlantic Cape Community College, where I spent ten years working with students across the full spectrum of the mathematics curriculum — from Introduction to Algebra all the way through Ordinary Differential Equations, Linear Algebra, and Discrete Mathematics. That breadth taught me that the core challenge is always the same: figure out where a student is, meet them there, and build a bridge to where they need to go. That's as true in a developmental algebra course as it is in a proof-based upper-division class.

Outside of the college classroom, I work as an instructor at Mathnasium, providing one-on-one math instruction to students from elementary arithmetic through Calculus I. That experience has sharpened my instinct for diagnosing exactly where a student's understanding breaks down — and for finding the right angle of explanation that finally makes something click. I bring that same awareness into every class I teach, even when I'm standing in front of thirty students at once.

I also draw heavily on my professional background. I've spent years working with real healthcare data — building analytics pipelines, running statistical models, and turning raw numbers into decisions that affect real people. When I'm teaching statistics or calculus, I don't have to reach for contrived textbook examples. I can talk about the actual moment I used a model to identify a clinical trend, or why understanding variance matters when you're reporting outcomes to a hospital system. Students respond to that. It makes the abstract feel worth learning.

What I want most from a classroom is for students to leave with more confidence than they arrived with. Not just competence — though that matters — but the sense that they are capable of hard thinking, that mathematics is something they can do. That's what keeps me teaching.
